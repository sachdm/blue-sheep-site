## 1. What does Will do for work?

Your instinct that "maybe it doesn't matter" is half right — but the job can do thematic work for free. Three options, strongest first:

**A. Identity verification / fraud ops.** Will's job is verifying that remote workers are who they say they are — KYC checks, liveness detection, flagging deepfaked interview candidates. This is the one with a plot engine in it: the scam in Mexico only works if it can fool people exactly like Will. He is the antibody, and the film moves him into the body. It also explains why the group might keep him alive longer than the others — he knows the detection systems. His expertise is the thing that damns him.

**B. He trains AI.** His day job is labeling data / writing feedback that improves the model that will replace him. The scam then isn't an aberration — it's just his job description taken to its logical end. "The human is kept to train the model, then discarded" becomes true on both sides of the border, legally in Ohio and criminally in Mexico City. Pure theme, less plot.

**C. Deliberately unexplainable.** Nobody in the office, including Will, can say what the company does. Title like "Workflow Integration Associate II." Feeds your line — "very soon none of us will know what we are doing anymore" — and makes the audience complicit: they can't say what he'd be losing. Funny, but gives Act 3 less to work with.

A and B can merge: fraud-ops teams are already half-automated; Will spends his days approving the AI's fraud calls. He rubber-stamps a machine that watches for people like the person he's about to become.

## 2. The Cairns problem — and the big option

Margin note: *"HAVE TO FIGURE OUT WHAT WILL is saying in response."*

Option one is craft-level: Will never gets to answer. The girls pull Cairns away, "love you buddy!!", screen stays on. The unanswered offer is the hook — Will's reply becomes the whole second act.

Option two is structural, and it's the one worth sitting with: **Cairns is already gone.** The FaceTime Cairns is the AI likeness. Reread Scene #1 with that in mind — the dialogue is already weirdly promotional ("everything is two for one," "life on crack," "say the word and I'll help you get set up"). It reads like an ad because it *is* one: the model running a recruitment script, generating a poolside paradise, two girls, bottomless margaritas. The scene you've written doesn't need to change a word — it just becomes retroactively horrifying on rewatch. The margin note "would be cool if they are all on a meeting and then they fall after that chat" fits here too: the call drops or glitches a beat too cleanly.

If Cairns is bait, then **Michelle is the pipeline** — the fixer who "sets people up down here" is the intake funnel for the scheme. The offer Will can't answer is the trap's front door.

## 3. John

Margin notes: *"dweeby but loyal"* and *"Cairns should be an employee aswell."*

John is your counterweight and he's already working. He's never left the state and it "doesn't sound too bad" — he's the one character with no lure, which makes him the only one who can see clearly. The mini-putt course in the abandoned office is a great image: John colonizes the dead office the way nomads colonize a dead neighborhood, except harmlessly, locally, with a friend. Consider: John is who the film thinks is right, and the film should be embarrassed about that, the way Will is embarrassed of him.

If Cairns was also an employee (all three at the same firm), the triangle sharpens: three responses to the same dying office — Cairns left loudly, John stayed happily, Will is suspended between them. And it gives the firm a reason to be in the story's endgame: the company keeps paying "Cairns" every two weeks and never notices.

## 4. Structure sketch

**Act 1 — The office.** Hollow HQ, robot boss with the monitor face, real boss feeding it broken sentences from home. Establish Will's job (see §1). Scene #1 lands here. The lure is planted. End on: something breaks — his unit goes remote, or his AI counterpart out-performs him — and Michelle's number is right there.

**Act 2 — Mexico City.** Honeymoon first: the nomad fantasy fully delivered, shot like the ads. Then the perspective starts flipping, your westerner/"foreigner" alternation: the barista priced out of her own neighborhood, the landlord's son who can't rent in the building his family owns, graffiti that Will can't read but we can. Onboarding oddities played as convenience: biometric scan "for the residency card," voice recording "for the building intercom," a contract selfie video. Midpoint: Will finds Cairns — the real one, or what's left of his situation — and understands the call from Scene #1 was the product.

**Act 3 — Inside the scheme.** Will is kept. His model trains on him; his firm back home never misses a paycheck cycle. The full flip completes: he is now the invisible worker with no identity, no papers, no name — the "foreigner" in the exact sense the political noise back home was about. The revolutionary group gets its case made — they're monetizing the colonizers, and the film should let that argument land before it shows the cost. His choice: join, escape, or the bleakest option — be promoted. The AI Will gets employee of the month.

**Bookend:** open and close on the robot boss's monitor face. First time it's absurd. Last time it's indistinguishable from everyone else on the call.
