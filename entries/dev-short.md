# NOMAD — The Short (Acts 1+2 compressed)

Thesis: Acts One and Two are a complete short because the Act Two button is a complete ending — the cycle closing on John. Act Three's questions (the real Cairns, who Michelle is) are the kind a short is allowed to leave open; in fact the ambiguity plays *stronger* at short length. Target: 15–20 minutes. The short doubles as the feature's proof-of-concept.

## The spine: three calls

Strip everything else away and the story is three video calls:

1. **Cairns sells Will.** (Scene #1, as handwritten — the centerpiece, virtually untouched.)
2. **Cairns fails Will.** (The midpoint tell — same poolside frame, one private-memory question, correct-but-transcribed answer, the eyes going nowhere.)
3. **"Will" sells John.** (The button — cadence perfect, slightly too on-message. John starts typing back.)

Same frame three times. The audience learns to read the frame, then the frame turns on them. Everything else in the short exists to load these three calls.

## Beat structure (~15–20 min)

**1. Cold open — Cairns leaves (90 sec).** Compressed to essentials: the box, ten seconds of the speech, the robot boss farewell ("I'll flag that upstream"), the elevator — NO PLAN — doors close mid-sell. Title card. One year later.

**2. The office (2–3 min).** One continuous morning: light-runway walk past Cairns' shrine desk, the 8:55 full pot, the queue of faces, APPROVE APPROVE APPROVE. Fold the erosion into ONE scene: the skill beat (he catches the synthetic, streak resets to zero) immediately followed by the metrics screen — system 99.1, Will 96.8, "top human performer." The whole four-beat erosion becomes one gut-punch juxtaposition: he's better than the machine in the one way the dashboard can't count, and the dashboard just demoted him for it.

**3. Call #1 — Cairns sells (3 min).** Scene #1 as written. John and the beers, "since when does Cairns have a plan," mini-putt, "never left the state."

**4. The dial + the move (90 sec).** Text to Michelle, instant dots, then the permission-screen montage at speed: passport scan, selfie video, voice enrollment, gait quiz. In a short this montage can be almost subliminal — the heist hiding inside a UI montage.

**5. Honeymoon (90 sec).** The ad come true, fridge stocked with his brands, pool party where he realizes he's inside the ad and toasts it. One subtitled local exchange he walks through — the alternation device planted once, cleanly.

**6. Call #2 — Cairns fails (2–3 min).** The tell. Four seconds of Will fully awake. He laughs it off, closes the laptop, sits still.

**7. The drained pool (2 min).** Skip the investigation sequence entirely — the short can jump straight from suspicion to the staging apartment: drained pool, ring lights, pressed loud shirts, the callback sheet dated eight months ago. Optional single glimpse through the service window: one row of workstations, one familiar laughing face 0.4 seconds late. No explanation.

**8. The handover (60 sec).** Michelle's text — appointment moved up. The clean room, the good light, the cameras he recognizes. Door closes politely. No violence, no struggle. A completion.

**9. Call #3 — "Will" sells John (60 sec).** Mini-putt course, phone buzz, the message. John, delighted, starts typing. CUT TO BLACK.

## What the short cuts (and the feature keeps)

The full four-beat erosion; Michelle as a developed presence (in the short she's three dots and one text); the westerner/foreigner alternation as a running structure (one plant only); the liveness-check sequence; the local thread; the operation's ideology; the real Cairns' fate; everything of Act Three.

## Why this cut works

- Nearly everything expensive is a location we already need: one office floor, one apartment, one rooftop pool (rented for a day — which is *literally the plot*), one staging room which is the same apartment redressed. The film's production constraints and its story are the same joke.
- Two of the three calls are the same set and setup. The whole short could shoot in a handful of days.
- The screens do the exposition. No one ever explains the scheme out loud; the audience assembles it from the three calls plus the drained pool, and audiences love being trusted like that.

## The award cut — no corners

Decision: NOT screen-life. Screen-life reads as a budget aesthetic at festivals and caps the ceiling. The film is shot classically, with cinema-grade craft, and the screens are rendered with total rigor inside that. The formal ambition lives in three devices, executed without exception:

**Device 1 — the three calls each have their own visual grammar.** Call #1 (Cairns sells): shot from Will's world, the screen small in his dark office — we watch him watch the ad. Call #2 (Cairns fails): the grammar tightens — the screen grows until Cairns' face fills the frame and WE are doing fraud-ops, studying eyes at cinema scale; the tell must work on a 40-foot screen with no dialogue underlining it. Call #3 ("Will" sells John): we never see the screen at all — only John's face lit by it, delighted. The film refuses to show us the fake because by now we'd believe it too. That refusal is the ending.

**Device 2 — the bilingual rule, kept ruthlessly.** Subtitled Spanish between locals, unsubtitled Spanish to Will, no exceptions, no cheating for clarity. And the local thread cannot be tourist wallpaper: bring on a Mexican co-writer and cast the barista/neighbor as a full performance, not texture. The film's argument is that Will can't hear the city — the film itself has to actually listen to it. Substantively and for credibility, this is non-negotiable.

**Device 3 — sound as the machine's fingerprint.** The 0.4-second delay is a rhythmic motif the sound design teaches the audience: the robot boss, the likeness's laugh, the pause before Michelle's dots. By Call #3 the audience hears the 0.4 seconds in "Will's" message rhythm without being told. Against it: the office's maintained sounds (the 8:55 pot, wheels on carpet, lights clicking on in sequence) versus Mexico City recorded full-spectrum and alive. The office is the only place in the film with dead air.

**The awards performance is Cairns.** One actor, two calibrated performances: the man (cold open — terrified under the volume) and the model's rendition of the man (correct, warm, a half-degree off in the eyes). The distance between them must be small enough to fool a friend and large enough to break your heart on rewatch. Cast for this, not for comedy.

**The heart, so it isn't a tech short.** Concept shorts don't win; grief shorts wearing concepts do. The engine is Will and John — the film is secretly about the friend who stays. John's "never left the state," the rigged hole 7, and his lit face in the final shot are the emotional spine. The last image is not a twist landing; it's a man about to lose his best friend a second time, typing happily. Hold on John. Cut to black before he sends.

**Production floor (what no-corners means concretely):** shoot the Mexico unit in Mexico City, not a substitute; real anamorphic night work for the honeymoon so the ad is genuinely beautiful (the trap must be the best-looking thing in the film — if the fantasy is cardboard, the film has no argument); the drained-pool staging room built as a meticulous 1:1 of the call frame so the audience's recognition is instant and silent; festival-grade color and mix. The office can be found for cheap — it's supposed to look maintained and dead. Spend where the film argues: the dream and the eyes.
