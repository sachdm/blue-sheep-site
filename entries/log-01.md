# Log 01 — Nomad

We now live in a world where you can live wherever you want and work, as long as you own a computer. For North Americans, Europeans, and Westerners in general, this presents an opportunity to take advantage of countries with a "weaker" dollar relative to their own. Hundreds of thousands of digital nomads flooded into Mexico City during and after Covid, as the modern workplace changed in front of our eyes — from in-person and communal to partially or fully remote.

From "our" perspective, this seems like a huge positive: we can set up a base in another country with a cheaper cost of living, likely rich with the cultural quirks that have been choked out at home by modern gentrification. From the perspective of the people in that country, the mass of digital nomads poses an existential threat to their way of life and their country's identity. This film aims to cover that tension, the same way Parasite highlighted the class divide in South Korea.

My idea is a character caught in that transition — compelled to follow others who faced the same choice, or "opportunity cost," in becoming digital nomads. Thematically, the story covers ground North Americans already recognize. It also acknowledges a duality: the experience of foreigners immigrating to the United States, and the political polarization around that subject — set against the hypocrisy of the same Americans who choose to move to a country that accepts these pools of non-tourist, non-working individuals throwing their dollars at an economy with no choice but to take the transactional benefits of digital-nomad spending.

While certain individuals profit off this boom of foreign wealth and interest, everyday citizens are pushed out to the fringe of the infrastructure that was supposed to serve them — and now betrays them. I find it hard to believe a revolutionary group wouldn't grow from this. In my imagination, that force would either be so "loud" that it topples the institution that betrayed them — overtly, in haste — or it would lurk quietly in the darkness, hiding in plain sight.

Everybody has heard of, or maybe even been caught in, a phishing scam. The idea is to take that to the max. In this world, an underground group has organized itself and built a scheme that lures in foreigners who come to live in Mexico and captures their identities. They keep them alive, feed them an AI-driven script, and use tools of deception to fool American firms into believing these workers are alive and well. The AI is trained on the likeness of the individual and does their work. The human is kept to "train" the model, then discarded once it reaches its final learning stage.

I envision meeting our protagonist still working at the office. He's one of the last few who still comes in every day — even his boss doesn't. Employees in his unit are the last to have an HQ that offers work in office at all. His boss is a robot with a monitor for a face that wheels around the floor; his real boss is at home, feeding broken sentences into an AI likeness that cleans up the lazy verbiage and handles facial expression and composure no matter his tone.

We first find his friend Will at his desk and see that he loves his space — it's well lived in, photos of friends and family everywhere. Among them is his friend Cairns, who is on a FaceTime call with Will right now. Cairns is thoroughly enjoying himself in Mexico City: by a pool, surrounded by food and drink, a beautiful girl at his side. The idea is obvious — plant the temptation in our protagonist's mind to follow suit.

The thing I need to figure out is what he does for work. But maybe that doesn't matter at all — maybe it feeds the idea that very soon, none of us will know what we're doing anymore. The goal is to convince our protagonist to move to Mexico and leave everything behind: to convince him he's sick of a gentrified world and craves the real-life experience still seemingly available in these other countries. Meanwhile, I want to show why the notion of becoming a digital nomad in good conscience is parasitic and colonial in nature. That is partially my goal.

I also want a lure aspect — the idea of temptation. On one hand, he's going and getting there; on the other, he has unknowingly walked into a trap. Throughout the film, the perspective shifts between the Westerner and the "foreigner" in a modern, technologically frightening work environment.
