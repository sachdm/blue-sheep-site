# NOMAD — Developing the Office

The office is Act 1's whole world, and it has to do two jobs at once: be funny enough that we enjoy being there, and dead enough that we understand why Will leaves. The comedy IS the horror — nothing here should be sad on purpose.

## The space

A floor built for 400, holding 9. Don't play it as ruins — play it as *maintained emptiness*. The cleaning crew still comes nightly, so 391 spotless desks. Motion-sensor lights that click on in sequence ahead of Will as he walks in, a runway for nobody. The coffee machine still auto-brews a full pot at 8:55 for the whole floor; Will drinks from it alone. HR still sends emails about fridge etiquette.

Geography worth keeping: the occupied desks cluster in one corner like survivors sharing body heat, while whole departments sit dark. John and Will's converted mini-putt room is deep in the dark zone — Sales, maybe, cables and monitors pushed against the walls, holes cut into the carpet. They've made something human out of the abandonment, which is the film's quiet counter-argument to Mexico City.

## The robot boss

Mechanics, so it stays consistent: a telepresence unit — monitor on a wheeled stand — with the boss's AI likeness on screen. The *real* boss is at home, typing half-sentences and fragments into his phone ("circle back re: the thing," "no on budget, soften it"), and the likeness expands them into warm, fluent management speak with perfect eye contact and a little head tilt. The likeness is *nicer* than the real boss ever was. People prefer it. That's the detail to protect: nobody resents the robot boss. They resent when the real boss occasionally shows up.

Comic rules that stay scary: the unit patrols on a schedule, so you can hear the wheels coming (the office's only jump-scare sound). It laughs at jokes exactly 0.4 seconds late. When the wifi drops, it freezes mid-sentence with its mouth open and everyone just... waits, politely, sometimes for minutes. Once, two units attend the same meeting because the boss double-booked himself.

## Will's job (locking in fraud ops)

Will's team verifies remote workers are real: liveness checks, voice matching, flagging deepfaked candidates in job interviews. But the team is half-automated — Will's actual day is clicking APPROVE on the AI's fraud determinations, a queue of faces, 40 seconds each. He's good at it. He has a streak going. The one time he overrides the machine (he's sure a face is fake; it isn't), he gets a gentle automated coaching module.

Seed for later: give him ONE moment of real skill in Act 1 — he catches a deepfake the system missed, off some human tell (the guy's eyes don't do the thing eyes do when you're actually remembering). Nobody cares much; it's a Tuesday. But the audience now knows what Will can see. Act 3 pays it: his own likeness will have to beat the tell he taught us.

## The trio at the firm

Making Cairns a former employee (per your margin note) completes it: three responses to the same dying office.

- **Cairns left loudly** — his desk is still there, untouched, becoming a little shrine. People still say "that's Cairns' desk" eight months later. His FaceTime calls come into this room, paradise projected into the fluorescence.
- **John stayed happily** — never left the state, built the mini-putt course, brings two beers. He's not in denial; he genuinely likes it here. The film should test him and let him pass.
- **Will is suspended** — comes in every day and can't say why. Not ambitious enough to leave loudly, not at peace enough to stay happily.

The Scene #1 call plays in the office, which you've already written (John holding the beers). Keep that: paradise is always experienced HERE, on a screen, at a desk. Will never sees Mexico with his own eyes until he's already inside the trap.

## The inciting crack — LOCKED: the erosion

**Decision: option 2.** Nobody fires Will. His queue gets rebalanced until he's watching something do his job. It's not a firing, it's an erosion — there's no one to be angry at, which is exactly the emptiness Cairns' margaritas are selling against.

(Option 3 — Will approves his own replacement through his own queue — is held as an Act 3 echo. It's too neat as a cause, perfect as a payoff.)

### Cairns left first — the erosion is a cycle

The erosion hit Cairns a year before the film starts. Same four beats, off-screen: rebalanced, congratulated, edge-cased, made Supervisor of nothing. He left at Beat 4 — loudly, because Cairns does everything loudly — and everyone called it a midlife thing. Will watched the whole process and told himself it wouldn't reach him.

What this buys: Cairns is the canary, not just the lure. The scheme in Mexico harvests people at exactly Beat 4 — Michelle's pipeline doesn't find adventurers, it finds the eroded, because that's when they're ripest. And Scene #1 recolors: "you gotta get out here bro" isn't bragging, it's Cairns (or his likeness) talking to a man standing exactly where he stood. The recruitment script works because it worked on him.

Optionally show it: a prologue or cold-open fragment of Cairns' last day — the desk being cleared, the loud goodbye — that plays as comedy the first time and as the top of a conveyor belt by Act 3. Will is not the first. He won't be the last.

### The erosion, beat by beat

This shouldn't be one scene. It's four small humiliations spread across Act 1, each played almost as a non-event, so the audience feels the water rising before Will names it.

**Beat 1 — The rebalance (invisible).** A system update note nobody reads: "Queue optimization rolling out this quarter." Will's queue drops from ~200 faces a day to ~120. He finishes early. He doesn't mind — he uses the extra hour for mini-putt with John. The trap of the erosion: at first, it feels like a gift.

**Beat 2 — The accuracy report.** Monthly team metrics, delivered warmly by the robot boss. The machine's fraud-call accuracy: 99.1%. Will's: 96.8%. The boss's likeness congratulates him — "top human performer!" The word *human* has quietly entered his job title. Nobody else notices. John, genuinely: "Dude, that's great."

**Beat 3 — The demotion that isn't one.** Queue drops to ~30 a day, all "edge cases" — the faces the machine is only 51% sure about. Meaning: Will now only sees the hard ones, so his accuracy *falls*. The system is feeding him its leftovers and grading him on them. He tries to explain this to the robot boss, who tilts its head, waits 0.4 seconds, and says it hears his frustration and will "flag it upstream." Cut to: the real boss's phone, the message summarized as "W. — morale."

**Beat 4 — Supervisor.** New title, tiny raise. His job is now a dashboard: watching the machine's decisions scroll by, empowered to intervene, structurally discouraged from ever doing so (every override triggers a coaching module). The queue is a river he stands next to. The scene to write here: Will clicking APPROVE on nothing, because there's nothing left to approve — he refreshes the empty queue the way you refresh a phone. THIS is the night Cairns calls. Scene #1 now lands on a man who spent eight hours watching a machine be him.

### Why this feeds the whole film

- The erosion is the legal, domestic version of the scam. In Ohio the machine replaces him slowly with a raise; in Mexico it replaces him fast with a pool. Same operation, different terms of service.
- His one real skill (the deepfake tell the machine misses — keep this beat BEFORE the accuracy report, so we know the metrics are lying about him) becomes the thing no dashboard can measure and the thing the Act 3 likeness must defeat.
- "Very soon none of us will know what we are doing anymore" stops being a line and becomes his literal job description: Supervisor of nothing, at 99.1% accuracy.

### The Michelle dial

End of Act 1: he doesn't dramatically quit. He opens the dashboard from home, watches the river run without him for a minute, and texts Michelle — something small and deniable like "hey, cairns gave me your number." The smallness is the point. Nobody storms out of an erosion; they drift out.

## Scene seeds

- Cold open candidate: the robot boss glides through the dark floor at 7 AM greeting empty desks by name — it hasn't been told about the layoffs, or it has and greeting desks is still in its routine.
- All-hands meeting: 9 people, 3 telepresence units, one guy on a phone propped against a mug. The margin-note idea — "they're all on a meeting and then they fall after that chat" — could live here: a call that ends and the office is somehow quieter than silence.
- John unveils hole 7 of the mini-putt course. It uses the CEO's old office door as a ramp. This is the happiest scene in the movie and it should ache a little.
- The coaching module scene: Will, alone, watching a chirpy compliance video titled "Trusting Your Tools," nodding along, dead-eyed, drinking the 8:55 coffee.

## What the office must accomplish before Act 2

By the time Will dials Michelle, the audience should agree with him. The office can't be a hellhole — it has to be a *reasonable* life that's evaporating politely, so that Cairns' pitch ("it's like life on crack") sounds less like greed and more like oxygen. If we'd take the offer too, the trap works on us.
